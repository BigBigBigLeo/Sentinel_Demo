Abstract—Given the rapid emergence and applications of
Large Language Models (LLMs) across various scientific fields,
insights regarding their applicability in agriculture are still only
partially explored. This paper conducts an in-depth review of
LLMs in agriculture, focusing on understanding how LLMs can
be developed and implemented to optimize agricultural pro-
cesses, increase efficiency, and reduce costs. Recent studies have
explored the capabilities of LLMs in agricultural information
processing and decision-making. Nevertheless, a comprehensive
understanding of the capabilities, challenges, limitations, and
future directions of LLMs in agricultural information processing
and application remains in its early stages. Such exploration is
essential to provide the community with a broader perspective
and clearer understanding of LLMs’ applications, serving as a
baseline for the current state and trends in the subject matter.
To bridge this gap, this survey reviews the progress of LLMs
and their utilization in agriculture, with an additional focus
on 11 key research questions (RQs), where 4 RQs are general
and 7 RQs are agriculture focused. By addressing these RQs,
this review outlines the current opportunities and challenges,
limitations, and future roadmap for LLMs in agriculture. The
findings indicate that multi-modal LLMs not only simplify
complex agricultural challenges but also significantly enhance
decision-making and improve the efficiency of agricultural image
processing. These advancements position LLMs as an essen-
tial tool for the future of farming. For continued research
and understanding, an organized and regularly updated list of
papers on LLMs is available at https://github.com/JiajiaLi04/
Multi-Modal-LLMs-in-Agriculture.

I. INTRODUCTION
Language processing refers to computational techniques
used to analyze, understand, and generate human language
using various algorithms and techniques including machine
learning (ML) [1], [2]. It encompasses tasks such as text
generation [3], sentiment analysis [4], and machine translation,
the automated conversion of text from one language to an-
other [5], enabling machines to interact with and comprehend
hu man language effectively [6], [7].Traditional Language
Models (LMs) have been foundational in language processing
as they provided a basis for text generation and understand-
ing [8]. With the advent of increased computational power,
advanced ML techniques, and access to large-scale data, the
emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs) has marked a
significant transition. These models, equipped with expansive
and diverse training data, have demonstrated an impressive
ability to simulate human linguistic capabilities, leading to
transformative changes across multiple domains, such as na-
tional security [9], healthcare [10], robotics [11], autonomous
vehicle technologies [12], road and traffic safety [13], security
and privacy [14] and so on [15].
In agriculture, the application of machine learning (ML)
and deep learning (DL) techniques has revolutionized various
aspects such as crop monitoring, pest management, and yield
prediction, establishing these fields as some of the most sig-
nificant beneficiaries of artificial intelligence (AI) technologies
[16], [17]. Among these technologies, LLMs such as the Gen-
erative Pre-trained Transformer (GPT) series have emerged as
groundbreaking tools [18], [19]. These models leverage deep
learning to process and analyze vast amounts of agricultural
data, offering insights previously unattainable through tradi-
tional techniques such as manual sampling, visual inspection,
and basic statistical analysis [18]. LLMs can respond to free-
text queries without training to specific tasks, demonstrating
remarkable adaptability and versatility. For instance, ChatGPT,
a prominent example of a generative AI chatbot, showcases
the potential of LLMs through its sophisticated fine-tuning

A. Method of Paper Selection
A systematic bibliographic analysis was employed to pro-
vide a comprehensive review of the integration of LLMs in
agriculture. The research process was structured around two
primary steps: the collection of relevant works and the detailed
review and analysis of these works.

which was then supplemented by secondary searches in five
additional scientific databases: ScienceDirect (204 papers),
IEEE Xplore (97 papers), PubMed (114 papers), Web of
Science (17 papers), and Scopus (28 papers), as illustrated
in Figure 2. The search utilized the query: [’Large Language
Models’ OR ’LLM’] in/and [’agriculture’], aiming to exclude
papers that referenced LLMs without directly applying them
to the agricultural domain. This initial search yielded a total

II. LARGE LANGUAGE MODELS
Language is fundamental to human communication and
self-expression, and therefore is a crucial method fo human-
machine interfacing [18]. The growing demand for machines
to perform complex language tasks has necessitated the de-
velopment of more advanced language models to understand

and generate language. The recent advances in language
models have been driven primarily by transformers, enhanced
computational power, and access to large-scale training data
[27]. These advancements have enabled LLMs to approxi-
mate human-level performance on various tasks. LLMs have
emerged as state-of-the-art artificial intelligence systems capa-
ble of processing text and generating coherent communication,
thereby generalizing across multiple tasks. The progress in
LLM has evolved from statistical to neural language modeling
and from pre-trained language models (PLMs) to the latest
state-of-the-art LLMs [28]. Traditional language modeling
techniques such as n-grams [29], Hidden Markov Models
(HMMs) [30], Maximum Entropy Models (MEMs) [31], Con-
ditional Random Fields (CRFs) [32], and Latent Dirichlet
Allocation (LDA) [33] have historically played a pivotal role
in natural language processing, laying the groundwork for
more sophisticated approaches like neural language modeling
and the development of pre-trained language models (PLMs).
These traditional language models only consider a limited
number of words or phrases at a time when analyzing text,
which means they struggle to understand relationships between
words that are far apart in a sentence or paragraph. This
limitation makes them less effective at understanding the
overall context or meaning of longer pieces of text.
While conventional language modeling trains task-specific
models in supervised settings, PLMs are trained in a self-
supervised setting on large corpora of text to learn a generic
representation that can be shared across various NLP tasks
[34]. With fine-tuning for downstream tasks, PLMs often
achieve performance gains superior to those obtained through
traditional language modeling [35]. As the PLMs grow larger,
their performance improves, leading to the transition from
PLMs to LLMs through a significant increase in model pa-
rameters and training datasets [36].
The core of LLMs is the Transformer architecture, which
has revolutionized the field of NLP through its self-attention
mechanisms and positional encodings, enabling the model
to capture complex relationships within data [37]. Although
these models build on foundational elements shared with
earlier architectures, the introduction of self-attention and
positional encodings marked a significant leap in handling
large datasets and scaling computational power. This scaling
has allowed LLMs to process and generate language with
remarkable accuracy, particularly in understanding long-range
dependencies in text [38]. The progression of NLP models
reflects these advances, with earlier models like Recurrent
Neural Networks (RNNs) and Convolutional Neural Networks
(CNNs) laying the groundwork, but struggling with capturing
long-range dependencies essential for tasks such as translation
and summarization [38], [39].
The breakthrough in addressing these limitations came in
2017 with Vaswani et al.’s introduction of the Transformer
model [37]. Its innovative self-attention mechanism enables it
to effectively process every token in the input sequence, cap-
turing long-range dependencies that earlier models could not.
This advancement significantly boosted performance across a
variety of NLP tasks [40], marking a major evolution in the
field. Figure 4 shows the history and evolution of LLMs.

A. Emergence to State-of-the-art and Capabilities of Large
Language Models
Building on the success of the Transformer, Devlin et al.
[40] introduced BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations
from Transformers) in 2018, which utilized deep bidirectional
training by conditioning on both left and right contexts,
simultaneously. This methodology enabled the model to be
fine-tuned with just one additional output layer to perform a
wide range of NLP tasks effectively, setting a new standard in
the field. The development of language models continued to
accelerate with the introduction of GPT-2 by Radford et al. in
2019, which employed a transformer-based architecture with
1.5 billion parameters, leveraging self-attention mechanisms
to enhance text generation capabilities [7], [38]. That same
year, the Megatron-LM was developed by Shoeybi et al. [41],
featuring 8.3 billion parameters which allowed for even more
complex pattern recognition and faster training due to its
innovative parallelization scheme.
In 2020, the release of GPT-3 by Brown et al. marked
a further leap in the scale and capabilities of LLMs, with
175 billion parameters, enabling high-quality text generation
with minimal fine-tuning [18]. This model not only pushed
the boundaries of what was possible in NLP but also set
the stage for the next generation of multimodal models.
Most recently, in 2023, OpenAI introduced GPT-4, which
can process both text and image inputs, demonstrating near-
human or superior performance on various professional and
academic benchmarks (OpenAI, 2023). Recently, Meta re-
leased the open-source LLM LLaMa, which, while smaller
in size, offers a valuable resource for researchers worldwide
(Meta, 2023). LLMs like OpenAI’s GPT-4 and Google’s PaLM
are at the forefront of AI, showing remarkable advancements
in handling complex tasks with vast parameter scales. These
models now support multimodal inputs, processing text, im-
ages, and sometimes audio, which broadens their applicability

in diverse sectors such as healthcare [42], finance [43], and
education [44]. Specifically, LLMs are revolutionizing content

interaction through chatbots, and personalizing educational

raises significant ethical concerns, including potential biases

with other AI technologies, promising even more sophisticated
autonomous systems. This ongoing evolution underscores the

of LLM technologies are realized responsibly and equitably.

3) LLMs for Precision Irrigation and Fertilization: The

technology. LLMs have demonstrated remarkable potential
in handling complex set of information, generating insights,
and providing sophisticated reasoning for decision-making
processes. Recent advancements have developed a sophisti-

and crop simulations using tools like DSSAT [136]. This
system employs deep RL, specifically a deep Q-network, to
train policies that interpret various state variables from the
simulator as observations. A key innovation is translating these
state variables into descriptive language, enhancing the LLMs’
ability to comprehend and optimize management practices.
Experimental results with maize crops in Florida (US) and
Zaragoza (Spain) demonstrate that the LLMs not only achieve
top performance across multiple metrics but also significantly
boost economic profits by over 49% while reducing environ-

The deployment of LLMs as RL agents has shown promis-
ing results in various domains, including robotics and user
interface interactions [137]–[139]. For instance, replacing tra-
ditional multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) with a distilled and
pre-trained BERT model as the RL agent has significantly
improved decision-making processes [140]. In this architec-
ture, the BERT model encodes state variable sentences into
feature embeddings, which are then transformed through fully
connected layers to align with the action space of the RL
agent. This setup ensures that the complex relationships within
crop management data are effectively captured and translated
into actionable insights. Another notable example is presented
in a framework that incorporates a powerful LLM with crop
simulations via tools such as DSSAT and Gym-DSSAT as
presented in Figure 6. In their approach, traditional numerical
state representations from simulation tools are transformed
into descriptive sentences. This transformation allows the use
of LLMs, which encode these sentences into embeddings
that capture a more nuanced understanding of the states. By
employing the advanced cognitive capabilities of LLMs, such
as their reasoning and memory functions [141], [142], the RL
agent within this framework can comprehend complex aspects
of crop growth and simulation environments more effectively.

Assist in Research Leadership and Interpretation: Tech-

providing expert guidance with a demonstrated 93% accu-

efficient management of data-driven tasks such as weather
forecasting, pest monitoring, and crop condition analysis.
LLMs can process and integrate multimodal data from various
sources like satellites and UAVs to aid precision farming [146].
Additionally, they can facilitate on-farm applied research by
using machine vision for pest identification and creating
actionable recommendations for TAs to convey to producers
[112]. By improving efficiency and extending their operational
capacity, TAs can better analyze trends and risks, ultimately
reaching a broader audience [128], [129].
Assist in Knowledge Translation and Content Creation:
LLMs significantly enhance the speed and accuracy of translat-
ing complex scientific knowledge into practical, personalized
advice for agricultural producers [93], [147]. These tools excel
in synthesizing insights from diverse data sources, enabling
TAs to provide comprehensive management recommendations
[104]. Studies in India and Nigeria show positive farmer recep-

management, with AI responses often surpassing those from
human TAs in terms of quality and timeliness [146], [148].
LLMs offer the additional benefit of multilingual translation,
facilitating immediate and specific advice tailored to individual
needs, which is essential in resource-limited settings [17],
[111], [112], [129].
Improve Work Efficiency and Processes: Often, TAs in
public and private sectors who work as consultants, Extension
advisors, or in other roles encounter various administrative
tasks that take away from their ability to build relationships,
learn, and communicate valuable technical information. LLMs
can help automate program scheduling, registration, record
keeping, drafting technical reports, follow-up emails, and
other administrative communication [149]. Increases in time
efficiency and savings will allow the TA to spend more time on
critical interactions and relationship building, allowing stake-
holder needs to be addressed more effectively. LLMs and other
AI applications create new opportunities for entrepreneurs
who wish to offer high-value and innovative solutions at
affordable prices, making research and data-driven practices
more accessible for a broader range of production operations
irrespective of their size, scale, and production type [146].
Potential Threats and Pitfalls: As we consider using LLMs
and other highly accessible AI tools to support and enhance
the work of TAs, we must also consider possible risks, pitfalls,
or threats. For example, LLMs are complex and require con-
siderable computing capabilities. Models must be continuously
trained and updated with new research findings, models, and
data, and the computational costs and complexity could limit
their widespread application, especially on highly specialized
issues where “general” GPTs are not entirely suitable [93].
To be fully trusted by both the TA and their stakeholders,
transparency is critical; LLM must be able to explain its
reasoning and limit its output to areas where queries can be
answered with a high degree of certainty, or there will be
opportunities to misuse these tools or generate recommenda-
tions that have unintended consequences [17], [149]. To be
effective, these tools will need the latest and highest quality
technical information as training data. This leads to questions
about data privacy, security, intellectual property concerns, and
prevention of unauthorized access to the raw training data
[93]. To the extent that LLMs are trained on available data
from geographic areas or groups that have the resources to
generate that data (time, expertise, financing, etc.), we must
recognize and understand that there will always be biases in
recommendations, potentially affecting producers and other

B. LLMs Integration with Agricultural Remote Sensing (RS)
Recent advancements underscore the pivotal role of remote
sensing (RS) in agricultural monitoring and management,
offering extensive applications from biomass estimation to
land use and land cover (LULC) changes [151]. LLMs con-
tribute to RS by improving plant canopy monitoring [152],
supporting agronomic practices [153], and refining PA systems
[154]. Key challenges such as spatial resolution and data
integration are addressed in literature, focusing on Sentinel-2’s
role in PA and decision-making [155]–[157]. Research extends
to RS applications in specialized settings like greenhouses
and plastic-mulched farms, and the broader implications for
sustainable agriculture under Agriculture 5.0 [158]–[160].
LLMs are increasingly integrated with RS, leveraging their
data processing capabilities alongside RS’s ability to gather
detailed spatial and spectral data, thus enhancing the scope
and accuracy of agricultural assessments [126], [161].
1) State-of-the-are LLM studies for Remote Sensing: Since
the inception of LLMs/VLMs, researchers started to find
applications in almost every field, including RS. For ex-
ample, Hu et al. [162] developed high-quality datasets and
developed a VLM tailored for remote sensing applications,
enhancing image understanding and reasoning capabilities.
Their research also emphasized the integration of additional
remote sensing data types and modalities to further improve
model performance. Osco et al. [161] used publicly available
datasets of satellite images to test capabilities; such as edge
detection, line detection, and image segmentation. They also
investigated the applicability of Visual ChatGPT in processing
remote sensing images. The research demonstrated potential
for various image processing tasks and provided an easy
interface for non-experts. Kuckreja et al. [163] developed
“GeoChat” that excels in multitask conversational capabilities
with high-resolution RS images. However, the model still had
room for improvement in handling small objects and multiple
object predictions. Zhang and Wang et al. [164] evaluated the
performance of multiple VLMs in scene understanding, local-
ization and counting, and change detection tasks using Earth
Observation data. Guo et al. [165] developed LLM-powered
system called “Remote Sensing GPT” that can understand user
requests, plan remote sensing tasks, execute these tasks, and
generate comprehensive responses to user-queries. This model
automated the task planning process, reducing the need for
human intervention and making remote sensing techniques
accessible to non-experts. However, it was limited by the
categories supported by existing remote sensing models.
Zhan et al. [166] developed ”SkyEyeGPT” for various RS
vision-language tasks, including image captioning, video cap-
tioning, visual question answering (VQA), and visual ground-
ing. Although the model showed promising results compared
to other models like GPT-4V, it struggled in addressing modal-
ity differences between satellite and aerial imagery to improve
VQA task performance. Silva et al. [167] developed a VLM,
named “RS-CapRet”, that can perform image captioning and
text-image retrieval for remote sensing images. The model uti-
lized recent advancements in transformer-based architectures
and vision encoders, handled multi-modal inputs effectively,
and could generate descriptions and retrieve images based on
user queries. Pang et al. [168] developed a large-scale dataset
of high-quality, detailed captions (HqDC-1.4M) for remote
sensing images, comprising 1.4 million image-caption pairs.
They proposed ”Helpful and Honest Remote Sensing Vi-
sion Language Model” (H2RSVLM) based on this dataset.
The model demonstrated exceptional performance on sev-
eral public remote sensing datasets and can identify and
decline to answer unanswerable questions, thereby effectively
reducing the generation of incorrect responses. Muhtar et
al. [169] proposed ”LLM-based Language Helps Remote Sens-
ing (LHRS)”, which provides a comprehensive understanding
of RS images and performs various tasks such as image
classification, visual question answering (VQA), and visual
grounding (i.e., locating specific objects within those images).
A ”BB (BaBy)-GeoGTP”, a model with GIS-specific knowl-
edge was introduced by Zhang et al. [170]. BB-GeoGPT was
designed to answer professional geospatial questions using
GIS-specific knowledge. This model can be used by GIS
researchers to gain insights and perform various geospatial
analyses. It approached the performance of commercially
available LLMs like GPT-3.5-turbo in certain domains such
as remote sensing and surveying. However, there is a need for
more training data and higher-quality instruction tuning data
for further enhancement. Zhang et al. [171] presented “Earth-
GPT”, a model aimed at integrating a variety of tasks, such
as scene classification, image captioning, region-level caption-
ing, visual question answering (VQA), visual grounding, and
object detection (both horizontal bounding box (HBB) and
oriented bounding box (OBB)) on RS images. Additionally,
researchers introduced a dataset named “MMRS-1M”, that
included over 1 million image-text pairs derived from 34
diverse RS datasets. This dataset featured multisensor images,
including optical, synthetic aperture radar (SAR), and infrared.
Earth-GPT demonstrated effective zero-shot reasoning and
generalization to new, unseen tasks and integrated coarse-scale
and fine-scale visual perception information effectively.
To improve the quality of the dataset, Xu et al. [172]
developed a high-quality, diverse, and unified multimodal
instruction-following dataset called “RS-GPT4V” for remote
sensing image (RSI) understanding, employing both GPT-4V
and existing datasets. This wave of LLMs and the sudden
surge in integrating LLMs and VLMs into remote sensing sig-
nificantly enhanced data analysis, interpretation accuracy, and
decision-making by automating complex tasks and identifying
patterns. This synergy has the potential to drive innovation in
environmental monitoring, disaster management, agriculture,
and urban planning.

IV. DISCUSSION ON RESEARCH QUESTIONS
RQ1: Which sources and types of data are used to
train LLMs for agricultural applications, and how are
they integrated? Large Language Models (LLMs) for agricul-
tural applications are trained on diverse and domain-specific
datasets. For instance, genomic data from 48 plant species
were utilized to train LLMs for genomic predictions [199],
while agricultural literature and FoodOn ontology were inte-
grated for food-nutrition matching tasks [108]. Additionally,
GPT-4 has been integrated with YOLOPC models to enhance
disease diagnostics by utilizing real-time sensor data and
market trends [102], [111]. Custom agricultural corpora, such
as those containing 30,000 sentences, have been employed to
improve named entity recognition (NER) models [109]. These
diverse data sources, including genomic datasets, sensor data,
and agricultural literature, form the foundation for training
LLMs, ensuring they are contextually aware and tailored to
specific agricultural needs. RQ2: What evaluation metrics
are utilized to assess the performance of LLM tasks in
agriculture? Evaluating the performance of LLMs in agricul-
tural applications involves various metrics tailored to specific
tasks. For example, the precision, recall, and F1-score metrics
were used to assess the performance of transformer-based deep
learning models for agricultural entity extraction [200]. In
genomic predictions, the accuracy of predictions was evaluated
using the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic
(AUROC) curve, which measures the ability of the model to
distinguish between classes, and the Area Under the Precision-
Recall Curve (AUPRC), which assesses the model’s precision
and recall capabilities [199]. The effectiveness of sentiment
analysis models employed for evaluating smart farming tech-
nologies, such as generative pre-trained transformer 2 (GPT-
2) and domain-specific BERT, was quantified using metrics
such as the macro-F1-score and accuracy, which assess overall
classification performance across various sentiment categories
[107]. Additionally, the effectiveness of NLP systems for
climate adaptation in agriculture was evaluated using domain-
specific criteria in a user study, demonstrating moderate agree-
ment among experts [130]. These metrics ensure that LLMs
are rigorously tested and validated across diverse agricultural
applications.
RQ3: What prompt engineering techniques are applied
to improve the performance of LLMs in agriculture?
Prompt engineering plays a crucial role in enhancing the
performance of LLMs in agricultural applications. Techniques
such as task-specific prompting and optimized answer align-
ment have been used to improve semantic understanding and
reasoning accuracy in agricultural text classification [100].
Custom prompts were also developed to enhance injury
surveillance reports, streamlining the data extraction process
[113]. Additionally, few-shot prompting was employed for
fine-tuning Multilingual BERT, enabling it to adapt to diverse
agricultural datasets with minimal training [201]. In another
approach, task-specific prompts were tailored to capture sen-
timent patterns in agricultural product news, significantly
improving the accuracy of sentiment analysis [114]. These
techniques underscore the importance of prompt engineering
in maximizing the efficacy of LLMs in various agricultural
contexts.
RQ4: Which optimization strategies are applied in
agriculture to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of
LLMs? Optimization strategies are essential for enhancing
the efficiency and effectiveness of LLMs in agriculture. Tech-
niques such as parameter-efficient fine-tuning using IA3 have
been applied in plant genomics, enabling broader genomic
predictions with minimal computational resources [199]. In
another study, a mask-based self-attention mechanism was uti-
lized to enhance global context understanding in sequence-to-
sequence learning for natural language interfaces in agriculture
[106]. Additionally, real-time monitoring and collaborative
dataset expansion were implemented to ensure system adapt-
ability in diverse agricultural settings, particularly in coffee
leaf disease remediation [26]. The use of transformer-based
deep learning models with Conditional Random Field (CRF)
for entity extraction has also shown improved performance
in agricultural tasks such as identifying crop types, disease
names, and agricultural chemicals [200]. These strategies
highlight the ongoing efforts to optimize LLMs for more
efficient and effective agricultural applications.
RQ5: What are the challenges and limitations of using
LLMs in agriculture? The deployment of LLMs in agri-
culture faces several challenges and limitations. One signif-
icant challenge is the scarcity of high-quality, domain-specific

training datasets, which affects the accuracy and reliability
of LLMs [106], [201]. Additionally, LLMs often encounter
difficulties in handling diverse and unstructured agricultural
data, such as injury surveillance reports or regional-specific
adaptation advice for climate resilience [130], [202]. Ethical
concerns, such as bias and intellectual property issues, also
pose limitations in the use of LLMs for agricultural safety
and health [203]. Furthermore, the high computational cost
and environmental impact of training large models, along with
challenges in real-time deployment on mobile devices, remain
critical barriers to their widespread adoption in agriculture

advancement of LLMs in the agricultural sector.
RQ6: What are the benefits of using LLMs instead of
traditional ML and DL methods in agriculture? LLMs
offer significant advantages over traditional machine learning
(ML) and deep learning (DL) methods in agriculture. One of
the key benefits is their ability to handle complex, context-
dependent tasks, such as semantic matching in food-nutrition
tasks or real-time decision-making in precision farming [108],
[111]. LLMs also provide more accurate and context-aware
solutions for tasks like agricultural entity extraction, where
traditional models might struggle with domain-specific ter-
minology [200]. Additionally, LLMs can leverage zero-shot
learning capabilities, enabling them to perform well on tasks
with limited labeled data, which is often a challenge in
agricultural applications [199]. The integration of LLMs with
other technologies, such as GPT-4 with YOLOPC for di-
agnostics, further enhances their utility by reducing model
parameters while improving diagnostic accuracy [102]. Fur-
thermore, LLMs are adept at processing multimodal inputs,
integrating data from diverse sources like satellite images,
sensor data, and textual information, which greatly enhances
the accuracy of predictions and insights in agriculture. This
ability to synthesize information from multiple modalities
allows for more robust and comprehensive decision-support
systems. By harnessing these capabilities, LLMs can provide
nuanced understandings of agricultural environments, aiding
in more precise and informed agricultural practices
RQ7: How do LLMs compare with ML and DL-
based approaches in their applications in agriculture?
LLMs have consistently outperformed traditional ML and DL-
based approaches across various agricultural applications. For
instance, the domain-specific pre-trained Agricultural BERT
model achieved higher macro-F1-score and accuracy in text
classification tasks compared to other transformer models
[107]. Similarly, LLMs demonstrated superior performance
in NER tasks for agricultural data, with models like GPT-4
and Multilingual BERT leading in accuracy and adaptability
[201]. In another study, LLMs outperformed traditional mod-
els in sentiment analysis of soybean-related news, achieving
high accuracy in predicting sentiment trends [114]. Moreover,
LLMs have shown greater efficiency in real-time agricul-
tural information diagnostics, significantly improving accuracy
with reduced computational resources [102]. These examples
highlight the effectiveness of LLMs in surpassing traditional
methods, making them invaluable tools in modern agricultural
research and applications.

V. MAJOR CHALLENGES AND LIMITATIONS
The implementation of LLMs in agriculture offers consider-
able potential but also faces a range of substantial challenges
and limitations. These limitations can be categorized, roughly,
into three primary areas: Data Quality Challenges, Data
Availability Challenges, and Other Challenges. These sectors
encompass a variety of obstacles that must be strategically
overcome to fully harness the potential of LLMs within the
agricultural context. A critical issue is the scarcity of domain-
specific, high-quality training data, such as gold standard cor-
pora, which significantly hampers the accuracy and reliability
of LLMs in real-world agricultural settings. Often, studies like
those focusing on agricultural term extraction or multilingual
text classification rely on silver standard corpora or limited
datasets, leading to potential biases and substantial gaps in
model performance [100], [205]. Moreover, LLMs frequently
struggle to handle the diverse and constantly evolving agri-
cultural terminologies, including synonyms, novel terms, and
context-specific jargon [205]. The complexity of agricultural
data, which spans textual reports to sensor data, requires robust
NLP models capable of interpreting rich contextual informa-
tion, a task that remains challenging [111], [113]. Additionally,
the high computational costs associated with training and fine-
tuning these models, combined with limited digital literacy
among end-users, particularly in rural areas, pose significant
barriers to the widespread adoption of LLM-based solutions in
agriculture [111]. Recent research has aimed to enhance the ca-
pabilities of LLMs in agriculture by tackling core challenges.
Studies have focused on semantic understanding in multi-
lingual contexts and genomic data prediction [100], [199].
Additionally, specialized fine-tuning strategies for agricultural
term extraction have made significant strides in addressing the
limitations of LLMs across diverse agricultural tasks [205].
These efforts are systematically detailed in Table ??, which
maps the specific research questions to their corresponding
challenges and studies, providing a comprehensive overview
of current advancements and ongoing research efforts.
A. Data Quality Challenge
The efficacy of LLMs in agriculture heavily depends on
the quality of data they process. Challenges in this category
include:
Inconsistent Data Formats: Agricultural data comes from
diverse sources like weather stations, satellite imagery, IoT
sensors, and manual logs, each using different formats and
standards. This heterogeneity in the data formats causes in-
tegration and processing challenges, making it difficult for
LLMs to synthesize information effectively [111].
Incomplete Data: Many agricultural datasets are incom-
plete, missing crucial information such as crop yields, nutrition
status or pest prevalence. This lack of comprehensive data
leads to gaps in the models’ knowledge, resulting in outputs
that might be imprecise, inaccurate or misleading [113].

Efforts to enhance data quality involve standardizing data
collection protocols, improving data integration through ad-
vanced fusion techniques, and rigorous validation and cleaning
to ensure accuracy and consistency. Collaborative efforts from
across the agricultural and tech communities are essential to
these endeavors [111], [113].
Improving data quality for LLMs in agriculture involves
several critical strategies. First, Standardization Efforts focus
on creating uniform data collection protocols and formats to
ensure consistency and ease data integration. This requires the
joint efforts of researchers, farmers, and technology develop-
ers. Data Enrichment methods, such as interpolation and fu-
sion, address data gaps and improve completeness by merging
information from various sources and including historical or
global datasets. Validation and cleaning are vital for ensuring
accuracy, utilizing both automated tools and manual reviews
to identify and correct errors. Finally, ”Crowdsourcing” and
”Collaboration” with local communities and experts can pro-
vide real-time data and refine data collection processes. These
approaches collectively enhance data quality, allowing LLMs
to offer more precise and actionable insights for agriculture.
B. Data Availability Challenge
LLMs require extensive, well-curated datasets to function
optimally, yet such data are often scarce in the agricultural
sector. The main issues include:
Geographical Variability: Agricultural data is highly vari-
able, and region- and crop-specific, making it difficult to gather
comprehensive datasets that are representative of different
agricultural practices and conditions [206].
Fragmented Data Sources: Data collection in agriculture is
often localized and fragmented, lacking the cohesion necessary
for effective LLM training. This is compounded by the lack of
standardized data collection methods across different regions
[21].
Addressing data availability challenges involves enhancing
the infrastructure for data collection, such as deploying IoT
devices and utilizing remote sensing technologies to gather
consistent and continuous data streams across diverse agricul-
tural settings [207]. Crowdsourcing and collaboration is vital
to address this challenge as well.
C. Other Challenges
Beyond data-related issues, several other challenges impede
the adoption and effectiveness of LLMs in agriculture:
High Computational Costs: The training and deployment
of LLMs require substantial computational resources, which
can be a barrier, particularly in developing regions with limited
access to such technologies [205].
Adaptation to Evolving Agricultural Practices: Agricul-
ture is a rapidly evolving field, and LLMs must continuously
adapt to new terms, practices, and conditions to remain rele-
vant and effective [199].
Integration Challenges: Incorporating LLMs into existing
agricultural systems poses significant technical challenges and
requires careful consideration of both technological and human
factors [100]. Educating farmers and other stakeholders includ-
ing demonstration of the economic impact of adopting these
technologies is crucial for wider adoption of the technologies
in subsistence and commercial farming.
Displacement of Agricultural Workforce: As LLMs be-
come more capable of performing complex tasks, such as
crop monitoring, disease diagnosis, and precision farming, the
demand for human labor in these areas may decrease [106],
[118], [147]. This technological advancement may lead to
the displacement of the agricultural workforce, particularly
in roles traditionally reliant on manual labor and decision-
making [23], [102], [109]. This displacement raises important
questions about the socio-economic impact on rural com-
munities and the need for strategies to retrain and upskill
the agricultural workforce [109], [110], [118]. Addressing
these concerns involves balancing the benefits of LLM-driven
automation with the potential consequences for employment,
ensuring that technological progress does not exacerbate social
inequalities.
Personal Agronomic Data Security and Proliferation of
Agronomic Misinformation: The use of AI solutions like
LLMs in agriculture presents notable challenges, particularly
in securing personal agronomic data and controlling the spread
of misinformation. LLMs handle extensive datasets that can
include sensitive information such as crop yields, soil health,
and farming methods. It is critical to secure these datasets to
prevent unauthorized access and data misuse [208]. Further-
more, LLMs have the potential to generate and disseminate
misleading agricultural advice, adversely affecting farming
decisions and outcomes [209]. An example of this can be
seen in the repercussions of Sri Lanka’s transition to organic
farming, where misguided policy decisions, potentially exacer-
bated by erroneous AI outputs, severely impacted agricultural
productivity and food security [210]. Ensuring the accuracy
and reliability of LLM-based recommendations, coupled with
robust data security measures, is crucial to mitigate these risks
and safeguard the integrity of agricultural decision-making
processes.
VI. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE ROAD-MAP OF LLMS IN
AGRICULTURE
It is expected that the future of LLMs in agriculture
will be marked by advancements in several critical domains.
For instance, integration of IoT and sensor data will enable
precise data collection, foundational for advanced predictive
analytics aimed at enhancing crop yield forecasts and opera-
tional efficiency. Automated crop management systems will
revolutionize traditional farming techniques, while tailored
agricultural advice and input recommendation will be cus-
tomized to individual farmer needs reaching to individual
plant level. AI-driven technologies will improve pest and
disease detection, offering early interventions to minimize the
loss while ensuring effective, integrated pest management.
Furthermore, optimization of the agricultural supply chain will
streamline operations from farm to market. Lastly, real-time
decision support systems will facilitate informed decision-
making, leveraging comprehensive data analysis facilitated by
LLMs.

As LLMs are integrated with general or proximal sensing,
and remote sensing in agriculture, they bring unique abilities
that enhance the analysis and use of vast agricultural data more
effectively.
A. General or Proximal Sensing Integration
LLMs-enabled proximal sensing will transform localized
agricultural practices by enabling more precise monitoring and
management directly at the crop or plant level. This integration
allows for real-time analysis of soil health, moisture levels, and
crop vitality through sensors deployed in the field. By process-
ing this data, LLMs can predict optimal planting times, cus-
tomize fertilizer applications, and manage irrigation systems
with unprecedented accuracy. For instance, by interpreting data
from soil sensors, LLMs can advise on the specific nutrients
required for different crop sections, optimizing growth condi-
tions and maximizing yield [211]. Additionally, these models
can enhance pest management strategies by analyzing images
from proximal sensors to detect early signs of infestation,
providing farmers with timely intervention methods [212].
B. Remote Sensing Integration
Conversely, the integration of LLMs with remote sensing
technologies, such as UAVs equipped with high-resolution
sensors, extends the reach of PA to wider scales. These
advanced aerial platforms can capture detailed imagery of
vast farming areas, assessing plant health, detecting disease
outbreaks, and monitoring environmental impacts across dif-
ferent terrains [213]. When combined with the analytical
capabilities of LLMs and Vision Language Models (VLMs),
the data harvested from remote sensing can be deeply analyzed
to provide comprehensive insights into crop status, growth
patterns, and potential stress factors. The synergy between
UAV technology and LLMs will not only streamline crop
surveillance and management but also empower farmers with
actionable, real-time data to make informed decisions on a
larger scale [214].
The integration of LLMs into agriculture represents a
paradigm shift in the way agricultural processes are managed
and optimized. Our comprehensive survey has delved into
various applications of LLMs, including crop production, pest
and disease detection, and remote sensing, demonstrating their
profound impact on enhancing efficiency, sustainability, and
productivity in the agricultural sector.
One of the key findings of our survey is the ability of
LLMs to process and analyze vast amounts of agricultural data.
For instance, LLMs have shown remarkable effectiveness in
crop management by predicting optimal planting schedules,
identifying nutrient deficiencies, and recommending precise
irrigation strategies. In pest and disease detection, models
like GPT-4 combined with CNNs have significantly improved

diagnostic accuracy while reducing computational complexity.
Moreover, the adaptability of LLMs to handle diverse data
types—ranging from textual descriptions to sensor data—has
facilitated the development of more accurate and context-
aware decision support systems. These systems not only assist
farmers in real-time problem-solving but also enhance the re-
sponsiveness of the agricultural supply chain, leading to better
management of resources and improved market outcomes.
Despite these advancements, our survey also identifies sev-
eral challenges that need to be addressed to fully realize the
potential of LLMs in agriculture. Data quality and availability
remain critical issues, as agricultural data is often sparse,
heterogeneous, and context-specific. Ensuring the reliability
and accuracy of the data used to train LLMs is paramount.
Additionally, the interpretability of LLM outputs poses a
significant challenge, as farmers and other stakeholders need to
understand and trust the recommendations provided by these
models.
Ethical considerations and sustainability are also crucial
aspects that require attention. The deployment of LLMs in
agriculture must be guided by principles that ensure fair
access, protect privacy, and promote environmental sustain-
ability. Addressing these ethical concerns will be essential to
gain widespread acceptance and to harness the full benefits of
these technologies.
The future roadmap for LLMs in agriculture is expected
to revolve around developing specialized, robust models adept
at handling multimodal data and providing real-time insights,
crucial for integrating LLMs effectively within existing agri-
cultural frameworks [215]. Increasing transparency and inter-
pretability is essential to build user trust and facilitate adoption
in decision-making. Moreover, the establishment of thorough
regulatory frameworks is imperative. These frameworks, cre-
ated through collaboration among researchers, industry stake-
holders, and policymakers, will ensure the ethical use of LLMs
and address intellectual property concerns [216]. Proactive
policy development is vital to manage LLMs’ impacts on
agriculture, aiming to enhance food system resilience and pre-
vent data exploitation. Ultimately, such strategic foresight will
enable the agricultural sector to leverage LLMs responsibly,
ensuring that their integration supports both innovation and
the equitable distribution of benefits.
